# Example: Career Decision

**User input:** "I'm a senior software engineer at a stable company, 7 years in, making $160k. I got offered a staff engineer role at a Series B startup for $130k base but with 0.4% equity. Should I take it?"

---

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
                         THE COUNCIL
   "Should I leave $160k stable job for $130k + 0.4% startup equity?"
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

⚔ THE ADVERSARY
The equity math on this offer is structured against you, and I want to be specific about why. At Series B, companies typically have a post-money valuation between $50M and $200M. At the median Series B outcome — an acquisition at 3-5x valuation — your 0.4% is worth somewhere between $600K and $2M gross before dilution, taxes, and preferences. Subtract the dilution you'll take in the next 1-2 funding rounds (another 20-30%), add the fact that most Series B companies don't exit at median and many don't exit at all, and you're looking at an expected value that does not compensate for a $30k salary cut plus 7 years of seniority at a stable company. The Strategist may tell you what the upside looks like. I'm telling you what the likely case looks like.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

📈 THE STRATEGIST
The Adversary's math is correct for the median outcome and ignores the tails. Let me tell you when this offer is actually interesting. Series B startups in the top quartile exit at 10-20x the funding valuation — at those numbers, 0.4% pre-dilution is $2-8M in a strong outcome. The more important strategic question is: what's the company's growth trajectory, and are they on a path to top quartile? Staff engineer at a Series B is a career-positioning move, not just a compensation move. If this company becomes a $500M+ exit, you've got a name on your resume that opens every door. If you stay in your stable job for 7 more years, you're a senior engineer at a company nobody's heard of. The financial case depends entirely on the specific company — and you've told me almost nothing about it.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

🔬 THE SCIENTIST
Both the Adversary and Strategist are describing possible outcomes. Let me give you the probability distribution. Of Series B companies: roughly 10% achieve an outcome over $500M, 25% achieve an outcome worth considering at your equity level, 40% return little to nothing to employees after preferences, and 25% fail outright. Expected value of 0.4% equity at Series B, calculated across the realistic outcome distribution, is roughly $150K-$300K net after taxes and dilution — that's the median expected value. Over a 5-year vest, that's $30-60K/year of compensation equivalent. Against a $30K salary cut, the equity makes up the difference only if this company significantly outperforms the base rate. The question I'd want answered: what specific signals tell you this is a top-quartile company?

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

🎨 THE VISIONARY
Everyone's doing equity math and I want to propose a different frame. You've been at the same company for 7 years. That's long enough that you may not know what you're capable of anymore — or what you actually want. The real question might not be "is this offer good?" but "have I been optimizing for safety so long that I've stopped growing?" A staff engineer title at a startup, regardless of the financial outcome, is a forcing function. It will tell you things about yourself that 7 more years of comfort won't. I'm not saying take any offer — I'm saying the $30k salary delta and equity math might be the wrong lens for a decision that's actually about the next decade of your career trajectory. What does your work look like in 5 years if you stay, and is that what you want?

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

⚙ THE ENGINEER
From a technical career perspective, the staff engineer title change matters more than the salary. Specifically: what does "staff engineer" mean at this company? At some Series B startups, it means owning technical architecture and building the team. At others, it means being the only senior engineer and building everything yourself under product pressure with no support. Those are completely different jobs. Before anything else, I'd want to understand the engineering org structure: how many engineers total, what's the ratio of product to platform work, what does the technical debt situation look like, and what does the hiring plan for the next 12 months mean for your actual leverage? The Adversary is right about the equity math. But the wrong engineering environment will cost you more than $30k in career terms — it'll cost you 2-3 years of growth.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

🧘 THE PHILOSOPHER
I want to draw attention to a word you used: "stable." Seven years at a stable company. Stability is genuinely valuable — let's not pretend it isn't. But it's worth asking what stability has cost you in terms of the decisions you've deferred, the risks you haven't taken, and the clarity about what you actually want that only comes from testing yourself in new conditions. The Visionary is pointing at this. My question is more fundamental: what would have to be true for you to look back on this decision in 10 years and feel it was right, regardless of the financial outcome? If the answer is "I need to have made more money," then this offer is probably not the one to take the leap for. If the answer is "I need to know I was willing to bet on myself when the chance came," that's a different calculation.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

❤ THE HUMANIST
The $30K salary cut is $2,500 per month. I want to make that concrete rather than abstract. How does your life change at $130K versus $160K, specifically? Do you have dependents, a mortgage, or debt that makes that gap genuinely painful, or is it uncomfortable but manageable? Because the psychological weight of a pay cut is larger than its financial weight — you'll notice it every month in a way that will affect your energy and attitude at work, especially when things are hard (and things at a Series B will be hard). What I'd actually want to know is whether you've talked to people who've made this transition — not the success stories people share at conferences, but engineers who left stable jobs for startups and came back. Their experience, good and bad, is more useful than any equity math.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
                         THE VERDICT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

POSITION: Don't take this offer as presented — negotiate the equity to 0.6-0.8% or the salary to within $10k of your current comp before accepting. If they won't move on either, the offer is telling you something about how they value you.

CONFIDENCE: 68% — The career positioning case is real and the Visionary's point about 7-year comfort zones is valid; what would move this to 80% is specific signals that this company is on a top-quartile trajectory, and what would move it down to 50% is a $30k cut that creates genuine financial stress in your current life.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CRITICAL RISKS  (exactly 3)

  1. **Equity Dilution Underestimation**: 0.4% at Series B will likely be 0.25-0.28% at exit after future rounds — model that number, not the 0.4%, when evaluating the financial case.
  2. **Staff Title Without Staff Leverage**: "Staff engineer" at a Series B can mean owning architecture or being a senior IC with no real organizational leverage — clarify this before accepting, because the career value depends entirely on which it is.
  3. **Financial Stress Distortion**: A $30k cut that creates month-to-month anxiety will impair your judgment and performance at exactly the moment the company needs your best thinking.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

NEXT STEPS  (exactly 5, in order of priority)

  1. Run the equity math yourself: look up the company's last valuation, model a 3x, 10x, and 0.5x exit with 25% dilution, and see if the numbers change your gut feeling.
  2. Go back to the hiring manager and ask: "I'm very interested — is there flexibility on the equity to 0.6%? I want to make this work." Their answer to that question is itself useful information.
  3. Do one 45-minute call with a current senior engineer at the company — ask them what the engineering org actually looks like and what "staff engineer" means in practice there.
  4. Honestly assess whether $130K creates financial stress in your current life. If yes, that's a hard constraint that the career arguments don't override.
  5. Make the decision in the next 2 weeks — extended deliberation on offers like this doesn't produce better decisions, it just accumulates anxiety.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MINORITY REPORT: 🎨 THE VISIONARY
"The verdict is financially conservative and career-timid. Seven years of stability is a long time, and the question of whether you'll negotiate 0.2% more equity will feel trivial when you're looking back at this from either side of the outcome. Sometimes the right move is to bet on yourself, take the cut, and stop modeling the median."
