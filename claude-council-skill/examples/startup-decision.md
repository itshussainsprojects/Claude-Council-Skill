# Example: Startup Decision

**User input:** "I have a SaaS idea for automating expense reporting for freelancers. I've been building it nights and weekends for 3 months. Should I quit my job and go full-time on this?"

---

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
                         THE COUNCIL
     "Should I quit my job to go full-time on my SaaS?"
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

⚔ THE ADVERSARY
Three months of nights-and-weekends building before talking to customers is exactly backwards. You've invested enough time to feel attached to this idea, but you still don't have one confirmed paying customer. Quitting your job now doesn't solve that problem — it just adds financial pressure to a validation problem. The base case isn't "it takes off slower than hoped." The base case is "freelancers already have enough solutions for this, and the ones who don't are price-sensitive enough that your unit economics never work." Prove me wrong by showing me 5 people who said "I would pay $X for this right now." Without that, nothing else in this conversation matters.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

📈 THE STRATEGIST
The Adversary is asking the right question, but I want to give the market context they skipped. Freelance and solo professional finance tools is a $4B market with 30% YoY growth, and the top players (FreshBooks, Wave) are still built around monthly subscription models that freelancers actively resent because their income is lumpy. There's a real gap for outcome-based pricing in this segment. The strategic question isn't whether the market exists — it's whether expense reporting is the right wedge into that market, or whether it's the feature, not the product. Three months of building nights-and-weekends also tells me you have execution conviction, which is rare. I'd want to know the distribution strategy before I'd endorse quitting.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

🔬 THE SCIENTIST
Let me put some numbers on the table. For B2C SaaS targeting freelancers, median time to $1K MRR is 14 months from full-time start. For a solo founder with no prior startup experience, roughly 22% reach $5K MRR within 24 months — that's the reference class. The more important empirical question: what's the current usage data on the product you've built? If you have beta users, what's the Day-30 retention? CAC versus LTV projections for this segment are notoriously bad because freelancer churn is high. The Adversary is right that customer validation is the critical missing input — not because it proves the idea works, but because it's the only data point that would meaningfully update my probability estimate upward.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

🎨 THE VISIONARY
Everyone's debating whether to quit now or later, and I think that's the wrong question. What if "full-time SaaS founder" isn't actually the right frame? You have a job, which means you have a network of employed professionals — who almost certainly have freelance colleagues who hate expense reporting. What if your go-to-market is selling this inside your current professional network as a concierge service first? Charge $200/month, onboard 10 clients manually, and learn what actually matters before you automate it. You don't need to quit to do that. You build the customer base while employed, and quit only when the side revenue crosses a threshold that makes the choice obvious, not courageous. The Adversary can't say that plan fails — they can only tell us if there's a reason the professional network approach doesn't work.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

⚙ THE ENGINEER
The Visionary's manual-first approach is sound, and I want to add an engineering dimension to it: the worst technical decision you could make right now is to keep building features before you've found the one feature that actually retains users. Three months of solo development almost certainly means architectural decisions that made sense in week 2 that will need to be undone at week 20. I'd put a hard freeze on feature development and spend the next 4 weeks on two things: instrumenting what you have so you can see what users actually do, and doing 10 customer interviews to find out what the one non-negotiable workflow is. The technical debt of building the wrong thing is worse than any code quality issue you currently have.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

🧘 THE PHILOSOPHER
The Engineer and the Adversary are both asking versions of the same question: is this viable? I want to ask a different one. You've spent 3 months building something alone, at night, while working a full day job. That's not nothing — it tells us something about your motivation that a market analysis can't. What I want to understand is: what does this mean to you beyond the business outcome? Because if the answer is "I want to build a successful business," then the others are giving you the right advice — validate first. But if the answer includes something like "I want to stop trading time for money" or "I want to build something that's mine," then the quitting question isn't really about this specific SaaS — it's about a life structure, and this product is the vehicle. Getting clear on that changes what decision you're actually making.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

❤ THE HUMANIST
I want to sit with what the Philosopher raised and make it concrete. You said you've been building nights and weekends for 3 months. I want to ask: what has that cost? Your sleep, your relationships, your free time? Because if you're already running on fumes while employed, quitting and going full-time doesn't relieve that — it adds financial anxiety to it. The people around you — partner, family, close friends — have been watching you do this. Have you had a real conversation with the person most affected by the quit decision about what the next 18 months actually looks like when it's hard? Not the optimistic version. The version where you're 12 months in, the growth is slower than expected, and you're wondering if you made a mistake. That conversation, and their honest answer, matters more than anything else in this analysis.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
                         THE VERDICT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

POSITION: Do not quit yet. Spend 60 days running the concierge approach while employed — if you reach 5 paying customers at any price, quit immediately. If you don't, the market has told you something important at zero personal cost.

CONFIDENCE: 74% — The market timing and founder conviction signals are both positive; what would move this to 85% is 3 customers who paid without being coaxed, and what would move it to 50% is a partner or family conversation that surfaces real financial incompatibility with the risk.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CRITICAL RISKS  (exactly 3)

  1. **Validation Paralysis**: The 60-day concierge window becomes an excuse to delay indefinitely — you need a hard commitment date: "If I don't have 5 customers by [date], I either quit or pivot the idea."
  2. **Undercapitalized Runway**: If you quit without a financial runway of at least 12 months of personal expenses, the stress of the quit will distort every product and pricing decision you make in the first year.
  3. **Feature Trap**: Three months of building means you're attached to what you've built. The concierge phase may reveal that the real product looks nothing like the current one — and you'll resist that signal.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

NEXT STEPS  (exactly 5, in order of priority)

  1. Do 10 customer discovery interviews with actual freelancers this week — not to pitch the product, but to understand what their expense reporting day looks like.
  2. Offer 3 freelancers a free 30-day manual concierge service: you do their expense reporting for them, using your tool where it helps and manually where it doesn't.
  3. After 30 days, charge the ones who found it valuable — even $50/month. Number of yeses is your signal.
  4. Have the "what does failure look like and are we okay with it?" conversation with your partner or closest advisor before making any quit decision.
  5. Set a hard decision date: in 60 days from today, you either have 5 customers (quit) or you don't (pivot the idea or continue as side project).

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MINORITY REPORT: ⚔ THE ADVERSARY
"The verdict is reasonable, but I want to flag that the 60-day concierge test only works if you actually charge people — free users tell you nothing. If you can't get a single person to pay you $20 for 30 days of manual expense management, the SaaS version isn't going to be easier to sell."
